As Programme Lead for AI and Machine Learning here at BPP University, "are you an AI optimist?" is a discussion I have with students all the time. Admittedly, the question tends to produce something more complicated than a simple yes or no.

"Yes, but…"

"No, unless…"

"Ask me again after I've finished this assignment."

That uncertainty is understandable. Conversations about AI have a tendency to jump between extremes. One minute, AI is going to cure disease, reverse climate change, and give everybody a three-day working week. The next, it is going to take your job, flood the internet with nonsense, and turn humanity into a minor inconvenience on the road to optimising paperclip production.

<aside class="de-key-term">
  <strong>Key term: Agentic AI</strong>
  <p>Rather than responding to one instruction, an agentic system can plan and carry out a sequence of actions towards a goal: searching for information, using tools, checking results, and deciding what to do next.</p>
</aside>

Computer scientists Scott Aaronson and Boaz Barak captured this range of possibilities in their essay *Five Worlds of AI*. These are deliberately extreme scenarios, and reality will probably borrow from several of them, but they reveal something important: technological capability does not, by itself, determine what kind of future we get. Technical choices matter, but so do political, economic, and cultural choices.

For the record, I am an AI optimist. I think AI is one of the three greatest innovations of the past 50 years, alongside the internet and those new washing-up-liquid bottles that can be squirted without leaking everywhere and becoming encrusted with green gunk.

But this article is not an attempt to settle every argument about AI. Instead, I want to ask a more practical question. Given the limited agency most of us have over the development of a global technology, how can we nudge things towards Futurama rather than AI-Dystopia?

## Start with the way we talk about it

Even the term *artificial intelligence* is slightly unhelpful. It suggests a synthetic competitor to human intelligence, something waiting on the substitutes' bench, ready to take our place. That is not a good way to win human friends.

"Copilot" is a more constructive metaphor. A copilot is capable and useful, but operates within a human-directed system. It assists, checks, advises, and occasionally spots something the pilot has missed. It does not get bored halfway across the Atlantic and decide that it has always wanted to run a cryptocurrency exchange.

Framing AI as a tool is not an attempt to minimise its risks. But framing changes the questions we ask.

<p class="de-reflection"><strong>Reflection:</strong> Which of your regular tasks would you trust an AI copilot to run end-to-end, and which would you still want to check yourself?</p>

## Do not ignore the jobs question

Understandably, much of the anxiety about AI concerns employment. This is not imaginary. UK research has identified administrative, secretarial, customer-service, and other back-office roles as particularly exposed to the first wave of generative AI. Entry-level work may also be vulnerable, because many of the tasks traditionally given to junior employees, drafting routine documents, processing information, producing basic code, or summarising material, are precisely the tasks AI can perform increasingly well.

The important word, however, is *tasks*. Jobs are bundles of activities. Technologies rarely arrive and remove every part of an occupation simultaneously. They automate some tasks, accelerate others, and create new ones. Research on the labour market remains mixed: there are signs of reduced hiring in some highly exposed occupations, but there is not yet clear evidence that AI has caused job losses across the UK economy at scale.

That is not a reason for complacency. It is a reason to prepare intelligently. Current AI systems are particularly good at three things:

<div class="de-glossary-strip">
  <details open>
    <summary>Summarisation</summary>
    <p>Compressing large volumes of information into more manageable forms.</p>
  </details>
  <details>
    <summary>Productivity assistance</summary>
    <p>Producing drafts, transforming formats, suggesting code, and reducing the time needed for routine cognitive work.</p>
  </details>
  <details>
    <summary>Agentic action</summary>
    <p>Planning and carrying out a sequence of actions towards a goal, rather than responding to a single instruction.</p>
  </details>
</div>

If machines become better at these activities, the safest response is not to compete with them on speed. Humans should strengthen the capabilities that complement them.

<section class="de-callout-grid">
  <article>
    <h3>Critical evaluation</h3>
    <p>AI can generate ten plausible solutions in seconds. Someone still needs to judge whether any of them should be implemented.</p>
  </article>
  <article>
    <h3>Empathy</h3>
    <p>Human problems arrive with emotions, relationships, and unstated needs attached. A technically correct response is not always a socially intelligent one.</p>
  </article>
  <article>
    <h3>Judgement and creativity</h3>
    <p>As machines handle more routine intellectual production, the distinctly human parts of professional work become more valuable, not less.</p>
  </article>
</section>

For students, this creates a real challenge. Traditionally, people developed judgement by performing junior tasks repeatedly. If AI removes some of those tasks, we will need new ways to build experience. Being able to generate competent work immediately is not the same as understanding a field deeply enough to recognise when that work is wrong. Use AI, certainly. But remain mentally present.

## Build technology that deserves optimism

There is also the question of what we, as technologists, choose to build. Thousands of apparently small engineering decisions eventually become the technological environment everybody else has to live in.

One constructive choice is to use **open models** where appropriate. The largest frontier models are extraordinarily capable, but they are also expensive to train and operate, concentrated in the hands of a few corporations, and often excessive for the problem at hand. Many applications can be built using smaller, open models, run locally, with fewer computational resources and greater control over data and behaviour.

That brings us to another principle: keep things simple. The technology industry has a longstanding problem with feature creep. Writer Cory Doctorow uses the term "enshittification" for the process by which digital platforms gradually become worse for their users as power becomes concentrated and value is redirected elsewhere. AI could accelerate this tendency, providing endless opportunities to add chatbots and automated "assistance" to products regardless of whether these things solve an actual problem. We should resist the urge to insert AI into everything simply because an investor, manager, or conference speaker has recently learned the word "agentic".

> Being able to generate competent work immediately is not the same as understanding the field deeply enough to recognise when that work is wrong.

One of my favourite AI-powered features is barely visible. When I swipe down on my iPhone's Home Screen, it predicts which application I am most likely to open based on time and location. It is a tiny service, but it regularly saves me several seconds of searching, and it does not announce itself with a metallic robot and the words POWERED BY REVOLUTIONARY ARTIFICIAL INTELLIGENCE. The AI is hidden inside a useful experience. That is probably what mature AI will look like: not a separate digital being demanding our attention, but a collection of well-designed capabilities that quietly make tools more helpful.

## Optimism is a choice, and a responsibility

I am a techno-optimist by nature. That does not mean believing technology will automatically make everything better. History provides far too much evidence to the contrary. Optimism means believing that better outcomes are possible, and then identifying the choices that make them more likely.

As students, educators, engineers, and users, we can frame AI as a tool rather than an opponent. We can develop human capabilities that complement automation rather than competing with it mechanically. We can choose open and proportionate technologies. We can build simple products that solve genuine problems. None of these choices will single-handedly guarantee a good outcome. But millions of small choices about how technology is built, taught, purchased, regulated, and used will shape the world in which it operates.

So, are you an AI optimist? Perhaps the most useful answer is not "yes" or "no", but: "I'm working on it".
