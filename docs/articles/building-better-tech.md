Working in tech, I'm surrounded by conversations about innovation every day. We talk about AI, automation, digital transformation, and emerging technologies. We talk about what the future might look like, how tech is changing the way we work, and the opportunities these developments create for businesses and society.

What we talk about a bit less often is the environmental impact of the tech we're building and using.

<aside class="de-key-term">
  <strong>Key term: Green computing</strong>
  <p>Reducing the environmental impact of computing systems through better energy efficiency, less waste, longer device lifecycles, and more sustainable hardware and software.</p>
</aside>

A few months ago, I delivered a BPP Masterclass on Sustainable Technology and Green Computing. Since then, the topics we discussed have felt ever more important as AI accelerates its presence in our lives and in the future of tech.

It feels as though the news is increasingly focused on both the rise of artificial intelligence and the rapid acceleration of climate change. Yet despite being two of the defining topics of our time, I don't think we spend enough time talking about how they link. Whilst many of us are aware of environmental challenges associated with industries like manufacturing, transport, and energy, the perception is often that tech is relatively low impact and "clean" in comparison. That may be because much of our digital presence feels invisible: it's all just in a "cloud", right? We stream films, store photos, ask AI tools questions, and access huge amounts of information instantaneously, without giving much thought to the infrastructure propping this up behind the scenes.

## The infrastructure behind the cloud

The reality is that every digital interaction relies on physical systems somewhere in the world. Data centres, servers, networks, devices, cooling systems, and energy infrastructure all support the tech we use every day.

One statistic that generated a lot of discussion during the masterclass was the amount of water used by large-scale data centres. According to the [Environmental and Energy Study Institute](https://www.eesi.org/), some large data centres can consume up to five million gallons of water per day for cooling. That's roughly the daily water consumption of a town of between 10,000 and 50,000 people. It's one of those figures that makes people stop and think, because most of us rarely connect our everyday digital activity with resource consumption on that scale.

## Tech is both part of the problem and part of the solution

<section class="de-callout-grid">
  <article>
    <h3>Where tech helps</h3>
    <p>Smart energy grids improve efficiency, data-driven agriculture reduces waste, renewable energy technology accelerates, and AI models climate scenarios and monitors ecosystems faster than we could otherwise manage.</p>
  </article>
  <article>
    <h3>Where tech costs</h3>
    <p>Every cloud platform, streaming service, AI application, and connected device sits on a network of physical systems that consume energy, water, and raw materials, whether or not we can see it.</p>
  </article>
</section>

We also know that the rapid growth of AI has brought greater visibility to these conversations. For a long time, discussions around computing infrastructure and data centres largely remained within technical circles, but AI has changed that. As millions of people use AI-powered tools day to day, there's growing awareness of the resources required to train, deploy, and operate these systems.

As someone who works closely with apprentices and postgraduate learners, I think this is one of the most important and timely conversations we can be having. Many of today's learners will go on to design systems, influence tech strategy, and shape how emerging technologies are adopted within organisations. Understanding sustainability isn't separate from technical expertise. It's increasingly part of what it means to be a responsible tech professional.

> Move fast and break things, as Zuckerberg famously lived by at Facebook, was fine when the thing you were breaking was a feature. I think we need a new motto for when the thing we are potentially breaking is the planet.

<p class="de-reflection"><strong>Reflection:</strong> Where in your own digital habits, or your organisation's tech stack, would it be uncomfortable to actually measure the environmental cost?</p>

## The e-waste problem

Alongside energy consumption, another challenge that deserves far more attention is electronic waste. Most of us have drawers stuffed with old phones, damaged tablets, forgotten charging cables, and devices we just stopped using. Individually these feel like nothing, but collectively they contribute to one of the fastest-growing waste streams in the world.

<div class="de-bar-chart" aria-label="Global e-waste generated, in millions of tonnes">
  <span style="--value: 76"><strong>2022 (actual)</strong><em>62m tonnes</em></span>
  <span style="--value: 100"><strong>2030 (projected)</strong><em>82m tonnes</em></span>
</div>

The United Nations reported that 62 million tonnes of e-waste were generated globally in 2022, with that figure expected to rise to around 82 million tonnes by 2030. What occurred to me while researching this topic was how quickly our relationship with tech has become focused on replacement rather than longevity. Each new device requires new raw materials, manufacturing, transportation, packaging, and eventually disposal, before the cycle starts again. Seen through that lens, sustainability becomes less about recycling and more about challenging ourselves to think differently about consumption.

## What we can actually do

The good news is there are practical things we can all do here.

<div class="de-chip-list" aria-label="Practical individual actions">
  <span>Buy refurbished devices</span>
  <span>Choose modular hardware</span>
  <span>Repair rather than replace</span>
  <span>Use recycling schemes</span>
  <span>Turn on power-saving settings</span>
  <span>Clear out unused cloud storage</span>
</div>

Even seemingly small actions have a cumulative impact. For organisations, though, sustainability mostly starts with something much more fundamental: measurement. You can't improve what you don't understand, and if organisations don't have visibility of their energy consumption, emissions, and environmental impacts, it's extremely difficult to make informed decisions about improvements. Increasingly, sustainability is moving beyond being viewed solely as an ethical obligation and becoming a genuine business priority, and I think that will only become more true in this age of AI.

## Reasons for optimism

Despite the scale of the challenge, this topic gave me reason to be optimistic. We're seeing continued advances in renewable energy, smarter energy management systems, battery innovation, precision agriculture, intelligent transport networks, and AI-powered environmental monitoring. Data centres themselves are evolving too, with increasing investment in renewable energy, more efficient cooling, lower-carbon construction materials, and creative solutions that repurpose excess heat for local communities.

These developments show that sustainability and innovation don't need to be competing priorities. In many cases, they actively reinforce one another.

## Why this belongs in tech education

Looking ahead, I think the future of sustainable tech ultimately comes down to people: the choices made by consumers, engineers, policymakers, educators, and technology professionals. That's why sustainable technology deserves a place in conversations about tech education. We rightly spend a great deal of time helping learners develop the technical skills needed to thrive in an evolving digital landscape, but future tech leaders also need to understand the wider impact of the systems they create. Whether someone goes on to become a software engineer, data scientist, tech consultant, or digital leader, sustainability is increasingly part of the context in which technology decisions are made. It's a thread we build into our **Level 7 Masters Data Science and Software Engineering** apprenticeship programmes, alongside the core technical skillset.

One thing I've always loved about the tech sector is that we are an industry full of creative problem solvers. If we can apply the same ingenuity to environmental challenges that we apply to technological ones, I genuinely believe our industry can become an even greater force for good.
