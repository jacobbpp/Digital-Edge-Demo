When I wrote about the Mythos issue, the point was not to panic. It was to pay attention to a simple shift. AI systems were becoming much better at finding weaknesses in software, working through complex technical tasks and operating at a level that made cyber capability feel less like a future risk and more like something arriving at speed.

<aside class="de-key-term">
  <strong>Key term: AI adoption</strong>
  <p>The process of bringing AI tools into real work, including the skills, safeguards, habits, and governance needed to use them responsibly.</p>
</aside>

Now Anthropic has launched Claude Fable 5, and the conversation has moved again. Fable matters because it extends that level of capability into something far more accessible. It supports longer-running, multi-step work, tool use and structured workflows, bringing this closer to everyday use.

This is no longer only a story about a powerful model being tested by researchers, cyber teams or carefully selected partners. It is about frontier AI becoming more available for software engineering, complex knowledge work, agentic tasks and business workflows.

That changes the meaning of the original Mythos warning. The warning light has not disappeared. It has moved closer to the dashboard most organisations are actually using.

## The next stage of AI adoption

In simple terms, this is the next stage of AI adoption. We are moving from tools that respond to prompts towards systems that can work across tasks, use tools, analyse information, check their own work and operate for longer periods inside structured workflows.

That is powerful. It is also exactly why the conversation needs to mature.

> A chatbot can be wrong in a paragraph. An agent can be wrong inside a process. That difference matters.

<p class="de-reflection"><strong>Reflection:</strong> What process in your organisation would become riskier if AI could act without someone checking the outcome?</p>

For businesses, the real question is not whether models like Fable are impressive. They clearly are. The more important question is whether organisations are ready for AI systems that can sit closer to the machinery of work.

If a model can help write code, analyse documents, coordinate tasks, inspect systems, support decisions and run through multi-stage work, then governance can no longer be treated as something separate from adoption.

## Capability alone is not transformation

This is where organisations need to avoid being dazzled by capability alone. A powerful model connected to messy processes, unclear permissions and poor data does not automatically create transformation. It can create faster confusion with a better interface.

The issue is not that organisations lack ambition. Most can already imagine use cases. Software teams want better code support. Operations teams want smoother workflows. Customer service teams want better knowledge systems. HR and finance teams want less manual checking. Leaders want speed, insight and productivity.

That sounds blunt, but it is where many AI initiatives risk stalling.

What data can the AI access? What is it allowed to do? Who checks its work? What happens when it is confidently wrong? Can users tell when safeguards have changed the answer? Can the organisation audit what happened? Who owns the decision when AI has influenced it?

These questions are not there to block progress. They are there to make progress usable.

## Safeguards need to be understood

The recent discussion around Fable’s safeguards is a useful reminder of this. Safeguards matter, especially when systems become more capable. But hidden or poorly explained safeguards also create a trust problem.

For example, when a response is redirected or altered due to a policy constraint and the user cannot see why, confidence erodes quickly.

Responsible AI is not only about preventing misuse. It is also about making systems understandable enough for people to work with them responsibly.

## This is an education issue

That is where this becomes an education issue again.

The original Mythos article argued that we cannot keep treating AI, cyber, governance and professional judgement as separate worlds. Fable makes that argument stronger.

If frontier AI is moving into everyday workflows, then learners need more than confidence with a prompt box. They need to understand the environment around the tool: the data, the process, the risk, the limits, and the points where human judgement needs to step in.

That applies to technical and non-technical learners.

## What this means for technical learners

For technical learners, the bar keeps rising. Code that works is not enough if it has been produced, modified or tested with AI support and nobody understands the security, maintainability or governance implications.

Learners need to be able to challenge AI-generated outputs, test properly, document decisions and explain why a solution is suitable for the environment it will operate in.

## What this means for non-technical learners

For non-technical learners, the need is just as real. Many of the most immediate uses of tools like Fable will not sit in specialist AI labs. They will appear in operations, administration, customer support, HR, finance, compliance and learning teams.

These are the places where people are already trying to reduce manual work, improve consistency and make sense of information spread across too many systems.

Those learners do not need to become AI engineers. But they do need a stronger baseline of digital judgement. They need to know how to use AI safely, assess outputs critically, avoid exposing sensitive information, support colleagues who are unsure, and recognise when automation introduces risk rather than value.

## Workforce development now matters more

This is why workforce development matters so much now. If these systems are becoming more capable, organisations need people who can translate that capability into safe, useful practice.

That does not happen automatically. It needs confident users, team champions, automation specialists, technical teams, managers and governance leads all pulling in the same direction.

That is where our **Level 3 AI Digital Champion** and **Level 4 AI Automation Specialist** sit so naturally.

## The role of the Level 3 AI Digital Champion

The **Level 3 AI Digital Champion** helps organisations build confident AI champions inside teams.

These are people who understand how work happens in their area, can use AI tools effectively, assess outputs critically, guide colleagues, reduce uncertainty and support responsible adoption in everyday workflows.

In a world where tools like Fable become more common, those trusted team-level champions become essential.

## The role of the Level 4 AI Automation Specialist

The **Level 4 AI Automation Specialist** then develops the next layer of capability.

It focuses on people who can look at business processes, identify where AI and automation can genuinely add value, consider governance and risk, work with stakeholders and help turn technological capability into measurable workplace improvement.

That progression matters because organisations will need both levels. They will need people who can support safe adoption close to the work, and people who can shape more structured automation and improvement across the organisation.

## The warning light is now inside the workflow

Fable does not make education less important because the tool is more powerful. It makes education more important for exactly that reason.

The future will not simply belong to organisations that have access to the strongest models. Access will matter, but it will not be enough.

The organisations that benefit most will be the ones that understand their processes, govern their data, support their people and build the judgement needed to use AI well.

Mythos was the warning light. Fable shows us that the warning light is now inside the workflow.

The question is no longer whether powerful AI is entering business workflows. It already has.

The question is whether organisations have the people, skills and confidence to use it wisely. That is where education has to respond with practical capability, responsible judgement and people who know what to do when the tools become powerful enough to matter.
