# Managing Context Switching

Context switching happens when you move from one task, system, conversation, or problem to another. Some switching is unavoidable, especially in busy technical teams. Too much of it makes work slower, more stressful, and easier to get wrong.

The aim is not to remove every interruption. The aim is to protect focus where it matters and recover quickly when you have to switch.

## The Big Idea

Every task has context: what you were trying to do, what you already checked, what you planned to do next, and what decisions were made. When you switch away, some of that context fades.

Good work habits make it easier to pause, return, and continue without repeating yourself or missing something important.

<section class="de-key-term">
  <h2>Key Term</h2>
  <p><strong>Context:</strong> the information you need to understand what is happening and what to do next. In technical work, context may live in tickets, notes, code, messages, meetings, screenshots, logs, and your own memory.</p>
</section>

## Protect Focus Before You Start

Before beginning a task that needs concentration, make the next block of work specific. A vague goal is easy to abandon. A clear next step is easier to resume.

<div class="de-check-card">
  <h2>Before a focus block, write:</h2>
  <label><input type="checkbox"> What task am I working on?</label>
  <label><input type="checkbox"> What does progress look like in the next hour?</label>
  <label><input type="checkbox"> Which files, systems, or notes do I need open?</label>
  <label><input type="checkbox"> What question am I trying to answer?</label>
  <label><input type="checkbox"> When will I check messages again?</label>
</div>

## Leave Yourself A Restart Note

If you need to stop, write a short restart note before switching away. This can save a lot of time later.

<div class="de-article-compare">
  <article>
    <h3>Hard to resume</h3>
    <p>Stopped halfway through the ticket.</p>
  </article>
  <article>
    <h3>Easy to resume</h3>
    <p>Form validation now works for empty values. Next: test long text, update the error message, then ask Sam to review the wording.</p>
  </article>
</div>

## Triage Interruptions

Not every interruption needs an immediate response. Some do. The skill is deciding which is which.

| Interruption | Possible response |
| --- | --- |
| Production system down | Stop and help if you are needed. Capture where you paused. |
| Quick question from a teammate | Reply if it unblocks them quickly, or suggest a time if it needs thought. |
| New task request | Add it to the task list and clarify priority before switching. |
| Interesting article or tool | Save it for later. Do not let curiosity steal planned work. |

## Make Switching Visible

If your priority changes, say so. This helps other people understand what will move and what may be delayed.

<div class="de-article-compare">
  <article>
    <h3>Unclear update</h3>
    <p>I am looking at something else now.</p>
  </article>
  <article>
    <h3>Clear update</h3>
    <p>I have paused the dashboard update to investigate the login issue. My restart note is in the ticket, and I expect to return to the dashboard after the support check.</p>
  </article>
</div>

## Recovery Routine

When you return to a task, avoid jumping straight into editing. Rebuild the context first.

<div class="de-process-steps">
  <article>
    <span>1</span>
    <h3>Read the restart note</h3>
    <p>Remind yourself where you stopped and what was next.</p>
  </article>
  <article>
    <span>2</span>
    <h3>Check recent changes</h3>
    <p>Look at files, notes, tickets, messages, or commits that changed while you were away.</p>
  </article>
  <article>
    <span>3</span>
    <h3>Run a small check</h3>
    <p>Confirm the current state before making more changes.</p>
  </article>
  <article>
    <span>4</span>
    <h3>Choose one next action</h3>
    <p>Restart with a small, clear step rather than trying to hold the whole task in memory.</p>
  </article>
</div>
